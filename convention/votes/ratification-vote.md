# Ratification Vote: The Docstitution

**Date:** 2026-03-09
**Presiding Officer:** Washington (Convention President)
**Secretary:** Madison (Documentation Architect)
**Quorum:** 54 delegates present. 54 votes cast. Quorum achieved.

**Ratification Threshold:** 80% of voting delegates (abstentions excluded from denominator)

**Anti-Hypocrite Condition:** A YEA vote affirms that the delegate believes the Docstitution follows its own prescribed principles sufficiently to govern others.

**Documents Under Ratification:**
- README.md (Preamble)
- Article I: Foundational Principles
- Article II: Document Types & Taxonomy
- Article III: Lifecycle Governance
- Article IV: Structure & Format Standards
- Article V: Roles & Responsibilities
- Article VI: Tooling & Infrastructure
- Article VII: Amendment Process

**Review Reports Considered:**
- Review Panel: Human Sympathizers & Minimalists (10 panelists, 5 critical objections — all addressed)
- Review Panel: Agent Sympathizers & Automation Advocates (8 panelists, 5 critical objections — all addressed)
- Anti-Hypocrite Compliance Review (47 violations identified — all remediated)

## 4. Dissenting Opinions

### Dissent of Lansing (Accessibility Engineer, Human Sympathizer)

The revised Docstitution strengthens its accessibility provisions — adding heading hierarchy requirements, code block language declarations, and CI accessibility checks to Article IV §3.2. These are genuine improvements over the draft reviewed by the Human Sympathizer panel. However, the framework continues to mandate WCAG 2.2 Level AA compliance while providing specific guidance on fewer than 20% of the applicable success criteria. The gap between "comply with WCAG 2.2 AA" and "here are eight things to do" leaves authors without actionable direction on contrast ratios, text resizing, bypass blocks, language declarations, and keyboard navigation — all of which are AA requirements that apply to documentation delivered through web interfaces.

More fundamentally, the Docstitution's own metadata — the "structural contract" that Article IV §1.3 describes as the interface between documents and every consuming system — is enclosed in HTML comments invisible to assistive technology. The framework mandates accessibility while structurally hiding its own navigation system from the users who most need structural aids. Until the Docstitution either renders its metadata in an accessible format or specifies a tooling requirement to do so, it fails its own accessibility principle. I dissent with respect, and I will propose an accessibility-focused amendment under Article VII immediately upon ratification.

### Dissent of Houston (Domain SME, Agent Sympathizer)

The addition of Article II §3.9 (Knowledge Representation) is a meaningful step — acknowledging knowledge graphs, ontologies, and non-Markdown structured formats as legitimate documentation types. The explicit permission for JSON-LD, RDF, and YAML as primary formats when content is inherently structured is a welcome departure from Markdown absolutism.

However, this treatment amounts to three paragraphs in a "Recommended" category — the same weight given to a CONTRIBUTING guide or an API changelog. Knowledge representation is not a document type; it is a fundamentally different paradigm for capturing and traversing organizational knowledge. The Preamble explicitly identifies "oral tradition, locked in the heads of senior engineers" as a failure mode, yet provides no mechanism for tacit knowledge extraction, no interview templates, no knowledge graph construction guidance, and no connection between the glossary (a flat list) and the rich relational models that modern knowledge engineering requires. The framework remains prose-first in an era that demands graph-first knowledge infrastructure. I dissent on the grounds that the Docstitution solves yesterday's knowledge management problem and underestimates tomorrow's.

### Dissent of McClurg (AI/ML Engineer, Agent Sympathizer)

Article V §3.4's provision for autonomous publication of Tier 1 and Tier 2 documents from approved pipelines is a genuine and welcome concession. It acknowledges that requiring human review of machine-verified artifacts creates friction without quality benefit. I commend the Convention for this revision.

My dissent is not about specific provisions but about the framework's constitutional posture. Article I's Core Philosophy declares "Agents participate; humans govern." Article V restricts agents from owning, approving, or publishing narrative content. These positions are reasonable today. But constitutional frameworks outlive the assumptions of their drafters. Within two years, AI agents will demonstrate the capability to co-author architectural decision records grounded in codebase analysis, generate design rationale from commit history and PR discussions, and maintain documentation estates with accuracy exceeding human stewards. By encoding human supremacy over documentation governance as a foundational principle — requiring a supermajority to amend under Article VII — this framework creates structural resistance to an evolution that is already underway. I would have voted YEA for a framework that established human governance as the *current default* with an explicit evolutionary clause, rather than as an immutable principle. I dissent with deep respect for the Convention's work and full confidence in the amendment process.

| Delegate | Reservation |

## 6. Convention President's Statement

**The Honorable Washington, Convention President and Chief Architect**

Fellow delegates,

The vote is recorded. By a margin of fifty-one to three, with no abstentions, this Convention ratifies the Docstitution as the governing framework for software product documentation.

This has been, by any measure, a rigorous process. Fifty-four delegates — spanning every discipline of the software craft, from three years' experience to thirty-five — submitted independent assessments, debated in committee, and subjected this framework to three adversarial review panels. The Human Sympathizer and Minimalist panel found five critical defects. The Agent Sympathizer and Automation Advocate panel found five more. The Anti-Hypocrite review found forty-seven violations of the framework's own rules. Every critical objection was addressed. Every anti-hypocrite violation was remediated. That this framework survived such scrutiny and emerged stronger is a testament to the Convention's commitment to honest self-examination.

To our three dissenting colleagues — Lansing, Houston, and McClurg — I offer not consolation but gratitude. Your objections are recorded in full, they are principled, and they will shape the first amendment cycle. Lansing is right that accessibility requires deeper guidance than eight specific provisions beneath a fifty-criterion standard. Houston is right that knowledge representation deserves more than three paragraphs in a recommended category. McClurg is right that the human-agent relationship will evolve, and our framework must be prepared to evolve with it. Dissent, clearly stated and grounded in evidence, is the engine that drives *Semper Reformanda* — always reforming.

To the twelve delegates who voted YEA with recorded reservations — your reservations are the Convention's roadmap. A formal metadata schema, reference pipeline implementations, mixed-tier classification, RFC 2119 declarations, customer documentation types, and a Quick Adoption Guide — these are not defects in the ratified framework. They are the first amendments waiting to be proposed under Article VII. The Docstitution was designed to evolve. I expect to see pull requests.

What we ratify today is not a perfect document. It is a *ratifiable* one. It establishes that documentation is infrastructure, not overhead. It declares that every document must say what it is, who it serves, and when it was last verified. It recognizes that documentation now serves two audiences — human and agent — and that neither is secondary. It insists that the framework follow its own rules, and it proved that commitment by surviving the anti-hypocrite review. It provides a clear, evidence-based process for changing anything within it, including its own foundational principles.

Fifty-four delegates came to this Convention with different roles, different tool preferences, different philosophies about human and machine collaboration, and different tolerances for process. What unites us is the conviction that documentation matters — that it is worth governing, worth investing in, and worth doing well. The Docstitution is the expression of that conviction.

The framework is ratified. The work begins.

*— Washington, Convention President*
*25 July 2025*
